Two Creative Briefs — L1 & L2
The live set is L2-only. These two briefs make June test the buyer-states we believe in—instead of confirming the one we already do.
WHY READ THIS
Finish this and you can brief two creatives that close the real June gap: a true L1 (category reclassification) and a true L2 built to the user-recognition standard—each with a kill line written before spend.
In 60 seconds
The live set does ONE job—explain commitment-escape (L2). It has no true L1, and the team keeps drifting back to L2 because the product pulls that way.
L1’s job is reclassification, not flexibility. The test: does the ad answer “which provider?” with “this isn’t the WiFi you’re comparing—it’s recharge-wala ghar ka net”? If its hook is “avoid the monthly,” it’s L2 wearing L1 clothes.
The L1 brief carries a hard guardrail: NO commitment/waste hook allowed. It must win on category reclassification alone—or we learn that L1 only converts on L2, which is itself a decision.
The L2 brief is built user-as-hero (situation first), to the standard of the split-light “zaroorat hai / zaroorat nahi” asset—the only live creative that triggers self-recognition, not just understanding.
Both: every headline lands on “net,” never “recharge.” We are NOT building L3 (mobile-data pain) this round—park any hotspot/top-up/“ek phone se poora ghar” concept.

Shared guardrails (both briefs)
GATE — non-negotiable
Destination noun is always “net” / “ghar ka net.” Never “recharge” as the final word. “Marzi se recharge karo” fails; re-land on net.
WiFi is a launchpad cue (earns the first 2 seconds), never the destination. The ad must not leave the user thinking “WiFi.”
NOT building L3 this round. If a concept’s core hook is hotspot-exhaustion, top-up fatigue, or “ek phone se poora ghar” — park it. This round is L1 and L2 only.
One dominant visual cue, one dominant message, one proof. Overloaded frames fail the 2-second test.
Brief 1 — L1: Category Reclassification
Not the WiFi you’re comparing. A different category. Win on that alone, or learn that you can’t.
The job: take an active home-WiFi shopper—someone choosing a provider this week, already comparing Jio/Airtel/local broadband—and reclassify the category in their head. Not “Wiom is cheaper / more flexible WiFi.” “Wiom isn’t the WiFi you’re comparing—it’s recharge-wala ghar ka net.” The differentiator (flexibility) comes AFTER the category lands, never as the hook.
RULE — the clean-test discipline
This creative may NOT use a commitment-escape / waste-avoidance hook (“poore mahine ka paisa kyun,” “mahine bhar ka recharge nahi”). That is the L2 engine. If we let it in, we never learn whether category-first wins on its own. The whole value of this brief is the isolation.
Direction to explore: capture search language as launchpad (“ghar ke liye WiFi dhoondh rahe ho?”) → reclassify (“ye wo WiFi nahi”) → land on “recharge-wala ghar ka net.”

Brief 2 — L2: User-Recognition (commitment-escape, built as hero)
Stop explaining the mechanic. Show the user their own life, and let them recognise themselves.
The job: take the blocked buyer—“I’d buy home internet but the monthly-commitment shape doesn’t fit my life”—and trigger self-recognition, not understanding. The live L2 set explains the mechanic (calendar, day-menu, billing-model contrast). This one opens on the user’s situation and lets the product be the resolution. Anchor: the split-light “zaroorat hai / zaroorat nahi” family scene—the one live asset where the user, not the product, is the subject.
RULE — the user-as-hero discipline
The user is the grammatical and emotional subject. Test: who is the subject of the sentence? “Wiom gives you flexible recharge” = product hero (fail). “You don’t need net all month—so don’t pay for all month” = user hero (pass).
Product enters only as the resolution, never the opening. Open on the tension (need vs no-need), let Wiom be what resolves it.
Make the on/off contrast (“zaroorat hai / zaroorat nahi”) the dominant, high-contrast element. That contrast IS the ad—if it’s missed at flash speed, it’s just a family photo.

The decision both briefs serve
These aren’t two creatives to add to the pile. They are the two experiments that make June’s learning honest. Right now the set can only teach us about L2. Run these and June answers two real questions: can L1 win on category-first without collapsing into WiFi, and does user-recognition beat product-explanation. Both have capped downside and structural upside. Both can fail cleanly—which is the point.
If the L1 brief fails its kill line, that is not a wasted test. It tells us the product’s true gravity is commitment-escape, and “category-first at L1” is a doctrine premise worth revisiting—the single most valuable thing June could surface.
